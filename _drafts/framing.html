"What are the consequences of the stories we tell? What do stories do? What do we do with the stories we tell? How do you change your story and how does that change the world? We often pretend that stories are givens, it's just that way. There are facts: measels are contageous, guns can kill people. But then there are [stories like]: you are my enemy, I'm right about this, there is only one way to do this [...] We've built these houses out of stories— take up residence in them. And in one way they're invivible to us. We don't say: 'I could be living in a completely different story'. And another way we see them, but see them as inevitable when we don't see ourselves as the storytellers. And so much, I think, of becoming free is becoming the storyteller of our own story; choosing to define who you are, what you're here for, and what your possibilities are."
- Rebecca Solnit talking about her book, The Faraway Nearby

Thought Technology

In the same way that all the man-made objects around us were dreamed up by someone, then designed, constructed, and transported to us, so were our ideas. Our worldviews are comprised of an overlapping mess of frames, a soup of stories that help us make sense of the world with ingredients we picked up from parents, teachers, politicians, and the billboard that caught you at just the right moment.

While shared frames are the beneficial fabric of our society, they are also sometimes contradictory, or fanciful, or past their expiration, and they're always incomplete because raw reality is full of hyperobjects our dinky brains are underequipped to comprehend.✶✶ If ever there was a sentiment that gets at my non-religious spirituality, it's this. We'll forever be blindfolded guessing at the elephant in front of us.

To add even more wrinkles, society is constantly trying to modify our frames or slyly slip another one on the pile. As Rebecca Solnit describes in the quote above, narrative framing is a neutral tool. It can be a statement of fact or it can be the arena in which an argument is made. Either way, it sets the bounds of the ideas we internalize that then shape how we perceive the world. The lenses we wear everyday that color our sense of what is possible.

A frame is also an angle. Situations and activities can be seen from multiple angles. This speaks to the slipperiness and subjectivity of reality, and underlines the importance of understanding how framing works. Especially when it's used as a tool for manipulation.

Manipulative framing is so pervasive that, without a proper defense, we often aren't aware that it's even happening. A secret, mind-altering spell. And because framing is a rhetorical device that shapes ideas, if it's successfully received, the frame is self-reproducing. By collectively acting and communicating within them, we reshape the world to fit our frames, sometimes at great cost to those that are unjustly on the wrong side of the shades.

Defensive Reframing

Thankfully we don't have to be passive in the framing process. We have the power to reframe as a defense against this rhetorical device. There are questions we can ask ourselves to get at framing when it may not be apparent. A counter spell, or a spell revealer at least. With practice, we can embed these questions as filters in our information intake process.

Examples of questions useful in reframing:

  • How does this person benefit from telling me this?
  • Does this statement only apply to a limited group?
  • Does this statement present the whole situation or just part?
  • Why is this information being shown to me?
  • Who benefits from me acting a certain way?
  • Who stands to gain from the options presented or excluded?
  • Who stands to gain from my work?
  • Will anything change if I do something different?
  • Who is in power in this situation?
  • Who is powerless in this situation?
  • Why is someone resisting a change?
  • Are there key words that may be misleading?
  • At what level in a process do I feel empowered to act?

The example statements with the manipulative framing exposed:

  • Healthcare should be available to all.
    Q: Are there key words that may be misleading?
    A: "Available" does not imply affordability
  • This is a transformative technology.
    Q: Does this statement only apply to a limited group?
    A: The technology will only be transformative to those that can afford it.
  • This company is a family.
    Q: Who benefits from me acting in a certain way?
    A: It's assumed that care and generosity come with a family. Employees approaching work with that attitude helps a workplace run smoothly. But in reality that care and generosity are an extremely conditional rarity when coming from the company back to the employees.
