Thought Technology

Framing is control. It's the arena in which an argument is made. It's the bounds of the ideas we internalize that then shape how we perceive the world. It's the lenses we wear everyday that color our sense of what is possible.

We are swimming in frames that orient our perspective in line with all kinds of ideologies. Situations and activities can be seen from multiple angles speaks to the slipperiness of reality, and underlines the importance of understanding how framing works.

An example: I give money to someone that is begging on the sidewalk. Two of the common ways of framing this act paint it positively (charity) or negatively (enabling)** There are, of course, other ways to view this but I wanted to choose two that people often encounter.. The charity framing comes from a worldview that acknowledges people fall on hard times and encourages helping in ways that we can. The enabling framing says that success comes to those that work hard to get it and giving someone money that is not working for it is hurting their chances at success.

Though I have strong opinions about them both, I won't get into the differences in ideologies here. The point is these are wildly different ways to view an action.

Even before we do the action, we have subconsciously considered the framings that are known to us and chosen an action that fits an ideology. There are of course other ways to frame this situation that could lead to very different actions. To list a few others:

Judgemental charity
I decide to only give this person food, because I worry they might use money to buy drugs or alcohol.
Non-judgmental inclusion / Mutual aid
I invite the person to the food pantry, church, or other community organization because that's where I have received support
Meritocratic paternalism
I tell them to stop being lazy and get a job
Non-judgmental curiosity
I stop to talk to the person to understand their situation with the idea that maybe I could more meaningfully help if I knew more
Busy; focused on self
I am too busy to do anything so I keep walking
Afraid; focused on self
I am afraid of this person so I uncomfortably keep walking

  • I invite the person to the food pantry, or church, or other community organization because that's where I have received support
  • I see this person as lazy and tell them to get a job

Clearly, not all framing is harmful to you or others, but it's important to be able to recognize when something is framed to manipulate us. Some reasons people choose manipulative framing:

  • lead us to a conclusion about an event
  • agreeing with a falsehood or partial truth
  • consenting to allow something to be done to us or on our behalf
  • buying something
  • voting for a candidate
  • choosing from an artificially limited set of options
  • leading us to obedience, conformity, or inaction

Manipulative framing is so frequent that, without a proper defense, we often aren't aware that it's even happening. And because framing is a rhetorical device that shapes ideas, if it is successfully received, the frame is self-reproducing. We all spread ideas and narratives that are framed through ideology by acting and communicating within the frame, reinforcing it.

Examples of statements that have manipulative framing:

  • Healthcare should be available to all.
  • This is a transformative technology.
  • This company is a family.

Defensive Reframing

Thankfully we are not passive in the framing process. We have the power to reframe as a defense against this rhetorical device. There are questions that we can ask ourselves to get at framing when it may not be apparent. With practice, we can embed these questions as filters in your information intake process.

Examples of questions useful in reframing:

  • Who owns this?
  • Does this statement only apply to a limited group?
  • Does this statement present the whole situation or just part?
  • Why is this information being shown to me?
  • Who benefits from me acting a certain way?
  • Who stands to gain from the options presented?
  • Who stands to gain from my work?
  • Will anything change if I do something different?
  • Who is in power in this situation?
  • Who is powerless in this situation?
  • Why is someone resisting a change?
  • Are there key words that may be misleading?
  • At what level in a process do I feel empowered to act?

The example statements with the manipulative framing exposed:

  • Healthcare should be available to all.
    Q: Are there key words that may be misleading?
    A: "Available" does not imply affordability
  • This is a transformative technology.
    Q: Does this statement only apply to a limited group?
    A: The technology will only be transformative to those that can afford it.
  • This company is a family.
    Q: Who benefits from me acting a certain way?
    A: It's assumed that care and generosity come with a family. Employees approaching work with that attitude helps a workplace run smoothly. But in reality that care and generosity are an extremely conditional rarity when coming from the company back to the employees.
