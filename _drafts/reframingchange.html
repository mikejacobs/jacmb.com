1. Change is ongoing and inevitable

Reframe the past, present, and future

Things haven't always been this way and they won't be this way forever. We don't have to work hard just to live in a world that prioritizes making money over our well-being. We aren't destined to struggle in the cyberpunk dystopias we see depicted in movies, TV, and video games. It may seem that way because we don't often encounter stories that tell of the future in an optimistic sense. I don't mean moralized stories where the difference is highlighted, I mean stories that have this optimism as the backdrop for a meaningful story. In cyberpunk movies we have some sort of tension and redemptive story arch. The same can be true of any future. :/

2. Shifting your perspective

It's a common mistake to think of change and power within the bounds of the systems we exist in and are familiar with: as an individual, as a citizen, as a worker, as a consumer. Each of those contexts have routes to change that is acceptable, and often the unacceptable routes are not clear until you find yourself on the wrong side of a power disparity. Our route to change as a US citizen means contacting a representative and voting. As an employee it means talking to your boss, joining a union, or possibly leaving a bad job. As a consumer it means contacting the company, buying a different product, or even advocating for others to boycott the product.

Where does our power end?

Power is real, though it's mostly not talked about explicitly in US society. But there is no escaping power disparities. The limits of our power, both perceived and actual, so strongly affect our reality that they define what we believe we can do; our ability to change the world; and what is possible if we work together. The sense of your own power is a powerful frame.

We all know that the boss has power to make decisions at work, and we have to follow orders. We all know that politicians have the power to make laws and enforce them through policing. We don't often talk about how we give that power to them, and if all chose to no longer give it to them, that power would evaporate.

That might seem naive or silly. It might seem obvious to you that this process of laws and enforcement is how we stay safe, or that bosses make decisions because they've earned the position or they're qualified. But, I encourage you to separate the needs for safety and leadership from the power to unilaterally decide what life and work look like for other people. This is how power disparities have been framed, for our protection, for our benefit.

The reality is we are manipulated into giving up our power. It may not feel like that's what has happened, because it's all we've ever known, and it's usually framed as the price of participation, or just the way things work. In exchange, we get access to money which gets us basic necessities we need to live.

The success of this manipulation also lies in the complete capturing of the mechanisms for change. Don't like the way a politician is doing something? Vote them out. Don't like a boss? Leave your job. Those might seem like freedoms, but they aren't actually fixes for a few reasons. Leaving a job isn't possible if your financial situation doesn't allow, as is true for most Americans. There is no alternative to the way government works, and it's been shown to prioritize the needs of those that fund campaigns. It makes little difference who is in office when the system inevitably makes them compromise for donations. When the proposed solution means more succumbing to the manipulation, it's not a solution. When the power disparity is part of a system, the system needs to be changed.

So, what if we start to extend our notion of change to look outside of these acceptable paths? This is what the David Graeber quote at the top is about.
