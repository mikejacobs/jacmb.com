I have found there are three essential parts to deeply internalizing that change is possible.

  1. Knowing that things haven't always been this way
  2. Shifting your perspective on what is possible
    • Which includes considering paths you might have rejected or been discouraged from following
  3. Setting your expectations: real change won't happen over night

Things haven't always been this way

I am currently obsessed with framing and reframing. In the above quote, Graeber does a fantastic job of expanding our definition of what is possible. The ultimate reframing is changing the bounds of what we think can be changed. I recommend reading the whole book because Graeber's sense of possiblity comes from a deep study of history. He tells of times when things were different, and times when people did recognize their ability to change the world-- to exercise their power. This is one of those thought technologies that has shifted my sense of self and my sense of power, and has led me on a path to change my priorities in life. It has given me hope, optimisim, and renewed interest in the world. I always found history boring until I realized that I felt empowered learning how things have not always been like this. Tremendously empowering.

Shifting your perspective

It's a common mistake to think of change and power within the bounds of the systems we exist in and are familiar with: as an individual, as a citizen, as a worker, as a consumer. Each of those contexts have routes to change that is acceptable, and often the unacceptable routes are not clear until you find yourself on the wrong side of a power disparity. Our route to change as a US citizen means contacting a representative and voting. As an employee it means talking to your boss, joining a union, or possibly leaving a bad job. As a consumer it means contacting the company, buying a different product, or even advocating for others to boycot the product.

But what do you do when your problem isn't fixed by those actions? What do you do when there are deep systemic problems

But those systems are what define the bounds of our power, and artificially limit or control it to create heirarchies used to direct our behavior and exploit us.

Where does our power end?

Power is real, though it's mostly not talked about explicitly in US society. But there is no escaping power disparities. The limits of our power, both perceived and actual, so strongly affect our reality that they define what we believe we can do; our ability to change the world; and what is possible if we work together. The sense of your own power is a powerful frame.

We all know that the boss has power to make decisions at work, and we have to follow orders. We all know that politicians have the power to make laws and enforce them through policing. We don't often talk about how we give that power to them, and if all chose to no longer give it to them, that power would evaporate.

That might seem naive or silly. It might seem obvious to you that this process of laws and enforcement is how we stay safe, or that bosses make decisions because they've earned the position or they're qualified. But, I encourage you to separate the needs for safety and leadership from the power to unilaterally decide what life and work look like for other people. This is how power disparities have been framed, for our protection, for our benefit.

The reality is we are manipulated into giving up our power. It may not feel like that's what has happened, because it's all we've ever known, and it's usually framed as the price of participation, or just the way things work. In exchange, we get access to money which gets us basic necessities we need to live.

The success of this manipulation also lies in the complete capturing of the mechanisms for change. Don't like the way a politician is doing something? Vote them out. Don't like a boss? Leave your job. Those might seem like freedoms, but they aren't acutally fixes for a few reasons. Leaving a job isn't possible if your financial situation doesn't allow, as is true for most Americans. There is no alternative to the way government works, and it's been shown to prioritize the needs of those that fund campaigns. It makes little difference who is in office when the system inevitably makes them compromise for donations. When the proposed solution means more succumbing to the manipulation, it's not a solution. When the power disparity is part of a system, the system needs to be changed.

So, what if we start to extend our notion of change to look outside of these acceptable paths? This is what the David Graeber quote at the top is about.
