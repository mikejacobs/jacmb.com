A loaded, non-trivial question

A friend recently sent me a post that was doing numbers in the tech startup and economics corners of social media that I generally steer clear of these days. I don't like embedding those things so here's the initial question, paraphrasing a screenshot of an economic study:

Why are worker’s cooperatives so rare? This is not a trivial question — because they can pay “dividends” in the form of wages, they can entirely avoid corporate income tax. They must be so inefficient by nature of their structure to outweigh their advantages.

And a follow-up with another screenshot from the study:

The key is that, since wages are set by vote, those workers who are below average are able to redistribute from the most productive to the least. Because you have to make a [tax] contribution, it is more expensive to leave — and so incentives are distorted

The short answer to the question: because worker co-ops genuinely run counter to the hegemonic power structure of the US. While we sing the virtues of democracy, tout the US as a democratic country, and do all kinds of imperialism to spread it to other countries, the governance model of our corporations— the driving force of our economic power, and often the most influential governing organization for most citizen's lives— is undeniably undemocratic.Closer to aristocratic or plutocratic. Co-ops, especially worker co-ops, change this.

Co-ops are operating in a hostile environment structurally, but the issues can be tracked to ideological and cultural hostility. Posts like the one we're dissecting are frequent and represent a majority view from those with money and power in the US. There are a number of overlapping reasons I could give to account for the lack of worker co-ops:

  • Being a lesser-traveled road, worker co-ops are more difficult to start and run
    • There are fewer examples to point to and learn from
    • Less knowledge to reference
    • Fewer businessy resources like helpful orgs, classes, incubators, etc.
    • There is less awareness that worker co-ops are even an option for doing businessI can't think of one movie or TV show that involves a worker co-op
  • Funding is difficult.
    • Investors aren't attracted to worker co-ops because the ownership structure doesn't allow for selling off big chunks
    • Investors also tend to want bigger investment returns than a worker co-op can promise
    • Banks sometimes offer loans but they tend to be smaller, that is if the bank doesn't hold the business cultural bias against worker co-ops
    • There are a few non-extractive funding options, but they are limited.
  • US culture's fervent individualism blinds us to the benefits of cooperative work.
    • We're still somehow holding onto meritocratic ideas of the American Dream
    • Great Man theory has a very strong presence in our views of human ability and progress,Along with Bad Apple theory. These two are used to entirely replace discussion of systemic influence.while there are few stories of collective struggle and success
    • Mainstream political views on both sides hold entrepeneurialism as virtuous without acknowledging the role of generational wealth and power
    • Cold War echos color co-ops as scary reds
  • US business and economics have a vested interest to scuttle worker co-ops.
    • They are inclined to believe that people are motivated purely by self-interest— the cynicism runs deep.
    • A viable, redistributive alternative is a threat to corporate hegemony.
    • Monopolists play dirty to crush small business, including co-ops

Unloading and reframing

Our post in focus lays out a multi-part theory in its case against worker co-ops:

  • Worker co-ops are rare because they're inefficient.
  • Specifically, the "nature of their structure" is inefficient, and this is a disadvantage. (I take this as an implicit comparison to the structure of corporations.)
  • The structure of worker co-ops allows them to democratically redistribute wages, removing the incentive for higher productivity.
  • Highly productive workers leave for a corp where they are better appreciated (compensated).
  • The increasingly inefficient worker co-op is unable to compete in the marketplace and fails.

There are a few juicy bits we can sink our teeth into (and tear apart) here:

  • Are worker co-ops efficient?
  • Is the democratic nature of worker co-ops inefficient?
  • Why do people choose to work at worker co-ops?
  • Why do people choose to leave worker co-ops?

Are worker co-ops efficient?

They must be so inefficient by nature of their structure to outweigh their advantages.

When an capitalist economist talks about efficiency, they are implicitly framing the conversation within the bounds of capitalism. A system that notoriously wealth with virtue.

Absolutely, worker co-ops are inefficient when compared to a corporation functioning in our economy... an economy that not only structurally favors corporations,More on this later but is overrun with backdealing and monopoly tactics that buy politicians and regulators to ensure their continued success. Is that the kind of efficiency we're talking about?

How much can we squeeze out of workers, while not sharing the juicy spoils, before they leave or revolt, oversqueezed? And what if we squeeze them just barely less than that for now, but try testing that boundary again next quarter when the workers might be more desperate and squeezable due to the measures we took to make sure that happens?

We all know this game. We're on the losing end of it perpetually. It is a cold, short-sighted trap that leads to us being treated as expendable machines. That's how things have been done in the US, quarter by quarter, since the 70s. It's how we got a graph like this:

The difference between the two lines contributes to profit— which is increasing at a wild rate! Over time, every hour worked produces more money for shareholders than the last hour, while paying the worker basically the same.

And this is just one graph, one way of squeezing us. The economists keep coming up with novel squeezing methods all the time. They have a word for this: incentives. Which we really need to call by its real name: coersion. Here are just a few coersive methods that are currently used at various levels of the economy to ensure we stay desperately employed and without power in our work:

  • Tying health insurance to work
  • Unforgiving, artificially inflated cost of living
  • Extreme resistence to unionizing
  • Interest rate hikes
  • Unclear costs in gig work
  • A lack of social safety net
  • The brutality and criminalization of homelessness

Cui bono?

So when I hear words like "efficiency", I ask: who benefits? who loses? and to what end? Corporations run on the exploitation mentioned above. Not as a byproduct, disparities in pay and power are central components of their innerworkings. The driving forces for work in this context are subjugation and coersion. Leading to a workplace filled with fear, uncertainty, apathy, and boredom. Whereas the co-op principles are decidedly human-centered. Inclusion, democracy, independence, accessibility, participation, autonomy, interdependence, community well-being. When work is no longer exploitative, when profit-maxxing is no longer the goal, work can be very different. It feels different to the workers. That's not to say some of these things can't be found in corporate workplaces, but in a worker co-op they are part of the mission. Focusing solely on the output, the efficiency, misses the real difference in quality of life— something economics is woefully inept at prioritizing or even measuring.

These assholes don't want people working at co-ops because it erodes their power.

A worker co-op is going to be more needs-based in its approach to compensation. One of the cooperatives I worked for set all full-time employee salaries at the same level, regardless of title, age, or experience. There were a few exceptions though. One worker's mother became ill and they suddenly had to support her financially. The culture was caring in such a way that this person felt comfortable bringing up the issue and the co-op voted to give them a raise.

My own story in co-ops aligns exactly with the highly productive worker that our quote above portends to defend. It's worth noting that my position at this co-op, which did have some levels of hierarchy, was in a high-level managerial role overseeing a department.

Why would anyone want to work at a worker co-op? Why would high-productivity workers want to?

The bounds of this argument are absurd.

We have already established that productivity-maxxing is not the top priority of a worker cooperative. So, if a worker happened to be highly productive and felt they should be compensated more. That would be a misunderstanding of the goals of the co-op?

The co-op principles are decidedly human-centered. Inclusion, democracy, independence, accessibility, participation, autonomy, sharing, community well-being... aren't these things worth more than even more profit?

So why would I, a high productivity worker want to work at a co-op?

Disengenuious and doing harm

Classic tactic of working to prevent the success of a thing and then declaring it an inherent failure. Holds up metric used to brutally enforce value extraction from labor and uses it to declare non-extractive labor a failure.

So, why are there so few co-ops?

Employee ownership, in the form of employee stock ownership programs (ESOPs), has been shown by other biz people to make corporations more productive and resilient. As a multi-ESOP shareholder, I can tell you it definitely helps.But an ESOP does not a worker co-op make. So, if employee-owners are more productive, but, according to the theory, worker-cooperatives are not... what's the difference?

So, if employee-owners are more productive, but, according to our quote worker-cooperatives are not, what's the difference? Democracy. Power. Control.

Better workers are incentivized to leave not because co-ops don't work, but because our economies are positioned against cooperatives. It's a failure in understanding the operating environment of the organizations. Pitting co-ops against corps in a system that favors corps and then declaring that co-ops don't work is not a true comparison. Also, the goals of organizations are often different. It's like saying non-profits don't work when compared to corps. Well, sure a corp might be more productive and generate more profit and growth, but that isn't the goal of the non-profit so why does it matter?

Reimagining

The economist sees the corporate workplace as one of benevolent meritocracy. The genius bosses overseeing the workers are deserving of their outsized ownership and rewards. The workers are lucky to have a job with their boss, as it grants them permission to live in society. Maybe they'll learn a thing or two, and a few exceptional underlings will nose-grindstone nose their grindstone their way to the boss chair in the future.

In a world where money gives you access to food, shelter, clothing, healthcare, and all sorts of other wonderful things and experiences— effectively your ability to exist —working for anything less than your maximum wage would not make sense. It would be irrational! How else would you live?

I would argue that view, for the average person, is a risky position to take. Sure, a small minority become moneyed gods. But if it's on you to accrue, then it's on you if you don't. Rolled a disability? Slipped on an addiction? Email scammer (another symptom of capitalist greed) got your savings? What if I told you there is a different path to those things you need and want? The Interdependence.

Have these people ever worked as a high-productivity individual in a profit-maxxing corporation? Sure, you can make a multiplier on your salary. Cadillac health insurance is a sweet ride, espeically for a grabbag of maladies like myself. Sure looks great from that perspective. But none of that accounts for the lived experience as a piddling resource, a lowly node on the org chart. The chart whose upper peaks teem with sleezoids and wannabe dictators, whose middle ranks are back stabbing body climbers. And that's just internally, the corpo sum-of-its-parts legally-people in the world is a sociopathic alien intelligence that is tearing our world apart.

Economics is terrible at accounting for these things: happiness, sense of purpose, empowerment, satisfaction, nature, "externalities". Not everything is about productivity and growth. Growth in the wrong way can be harmful. When this happens in our body, we call it cancer. Economics is a science in the same way basketball is maths. basketball commentary is a sport. In the end it's trying to rationalize and systematize a complex game that runs on human ability and feelings.

The sad reality is that people still live in a world where money is still their key to freedom. My broad criticism of economics is that not everything is about productivity. We have seen this blind adherence to profit maximizing destroy our world. It generates more profit to generate a cheap thing and deal with the reprocutions than to focus on quality. Knock-off tourniquets on Amazon selling for $2 a pop with a high failure rate (failure in this context often means death of the person wearing the device). People are not rational actors as economics sees them. And within that I stick out as a non-rational actor. yesssss

As a high-productivity individual, is it irrational to say that I'd rather prioritize happiness, positive impact, and mutual assured survival for my community over salary-maxxing?

Am I irrational for wanting to work without declaring dictatorial control and extracting value from my colleagues? Instead of building a moat and competing with the business down the street, wouldn't it be rational to build interdependence, share knowledge and resources, and make decisions democratically?

According to numerous academic studies, companies where at least 30% of the shares are owned by a broad-based group of employees, where all employees have access to ownership, and where the concentration of ownership is limited are more productive, grow faster, and are less likely to go out of business than their counterparts. According to recent research by the National Center for Employee Ownership, employee-owners have higher wages and net worths, receive better benefits, and are less likely to lose jobs to cuts and outsourcing during a downturn, compared with workers who don’t have ownership stakes in their organizations.
